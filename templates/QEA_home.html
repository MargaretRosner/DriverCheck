THE PROJECT

Why Passive Walking?

Passive Dynamic walking is defined as the dynamical behavior of walking when not drawing energy from a supply. Passive walking in its simplest form is a multi-legged system that once started on a slope can fall into a steady gait. It is the most efficient way to mimic a human’s gait because the mechanism is propelled using only gravity. By understanding the dynamics of this system one is able to harvest the “free” energy of gravity and integrate it into controllers to create the most efficient walking systems. These lessons learned can be applied to areas such as lower limb prosthetics and robotics.

We chose to build and model the dynamics of a passive dynamic walker for our final QEA project because we wanted to gain experience in dynamics, simulations and physical modeling. Additionally we wanted to do a project that could connect us to our target audience, the QEA class of next year. This project was a fantastic learning experience for both of the group members. We were able to take a deep dive into dynamics, find resources independently, learn modeling/simulations and more. This would be a fantastic project for future students to do. We hope by demonstrating our interpretation of the project and by thoroughly documenting our process we will be able to influence future years!

Our MVP

Our MVP is a both a physical and simulation model of a 2D parallel straight legged dynamic walker that represents human legs without knees. Our model can propel itself down a shallow slope around 6 degrees using only gravity. We aimed to meet this scope of MVP due to the short time frame for the project.

There are many fascinating directions one could go in with a passive walkers. One could add an additional pendulum on each leg (essentially adding a knee); one could focus on the inertia matrix of the problem and experiment with only having 2 legs and have the mechanism wobble; or one introduce springs into the ankles, hips, or knees and achieve a steady gait on a flat surface. Research

We began this project by devoting our time to researching the project. We read multiple papers about the Passive Dynamic Walker problem to understand the different approaches people had taken in the past. We first wanted to understand this problem in the frame of the original paper, Walking control of a semi-passive biped robot based on repetitive control algorithm written by McGeer (links and citations can be found in the citations section below). McGeer is credited for proving the usefulness of this dynamics projects and most passive walkers are derived in some manner from his paper. In the paper McGeer covers the difference static and dynamic walking, the shape and purpose feet, and steady walking for a 2D Biped Walker. We were able to use this paper to develop an understanding of the inertia tensor matrix and the importance of “rimless” wheels (aka feet of the machine).

Since 1990, when McGeer’s paper was published, many universities and researchers have published papers on this fascinating project. After reviewing some of these papers we decided to learn Lagrangian mechanics so that we could set up our project without relying on coordinate systems. This was invaluable because this let us evaluate angular motions, and x-y translation at the same time. We were additionally able to use Hamiltonian mechanics as a validation test that our energy was conserved throughout our calculations.

After learning Lagrange mechanics (see the “Overnight Assignment” link) we began to take a deeper dive into the approach the papers took to calculating a stable system. We began to analyze the dynamics in Walking control of a semi-passive biped robot based on repetitive control algorithm written by Yali Zhou and Qizhi Zhang. We rederived the dynamics of the swing phase equations presented in the paper and were able to gain a deeper understanding for how to approach the reference frames of our system and the connection between the energies of each moving object and the Lagrange equation. The paper additionally presented a new angle we could analyze the project from; this paper focused on creating a Biped walker that could walk on flat ground by placing a spring in between the two legs. This was helpful in understanding how to approach collision transformations, and provided clear base parameter for modeling.

The final paper that we did through analysis of was The Simplest Walking Model: Stability, Complexity, and Scaling written by Garcia. This paper was an ideal source to develop our understanding of the system we planned to create. The paper demonstrated a simple 2D biped walking down a slope. It offered an important insight into how to incorporate the angle of the ramp into our problem. Additionally the section on collision transformation at heel strike was another perspective of the problem and helped us derive our collision equations. The paper additionally went through understanding gait cycles which helped us understand the stability evaluation of passive walkers.

Deriving Equations of Motion

To derive the equations of motion for our system we chose to use Lagrangian mechanics. Part of the motivation behind the use of the Euler Lagrange equations to model our passive walker system was to make it easier to describe the motion of the walker using polar coordinates.

Once we had defined the movement of our two rotating bodies (the swing foot and hip) in polar coordinates we derived the kinetic and potential energy equations for the swing phase. The energy equations factored in the slope of the ramp but did not include the change in height of the walker in the global frame as it traveled down the ramp. We determined that the energy artificially gained by the height remaining the same as the machine walked down would be negligible when combined with the energy loss from friction between the ramp and the foot as well as energy lost during heel strike. After we had determined the energy equation we used the Euler Lagrange equation to derive the equations of motion for the model which resulted in two governing equations for angle Theta and Phi.

Simulation

Due to the nature of this project we decided to create a simulation. This would allow us to have a visualization of our system’s output based on multiple varying parameters. This would both allow us to test our project in hundreds over variations but also provide an intuitive presentation of how the dynamics affect the physical system.

We first approached the simulating our system by modeling one of the example problems we had worked through while learning Lagrange in order to understand the steps that go into creating a Mathmatica simulation. After gaining a basic understanding of simulations by modeling first, a single pendulum and then, a double pendulum, we were able to attempt to model the machine in, Walking control of a semi-passive biped robot based on repetitive control algorithm. By using the equations of motion we had previously rederived from this paper we were able to calculate the Lagrangian for a single swing phase. We were able to then simulate the passive walker taking a single step forward.

We then used this simulation as a base for our simulation and substituted in the equations of motion we had derived for a pendulum on a slope. We then simulated the walked on a slope. We finally added in a second step; this included adding our collision transformation matrix and changing the origin of the rotated reference frame to always be around the plant foot. We were then able to manipulate the visual output of the simulation to model these changes. We then incorporated a loop that allowed us to step multiple times and began experimenting with parameters. We were also able to derive conclusive motion graphs of the parameters as they changed over time.

Fabrication

One of the challenges of creating a physical model as part of our final product for this project was the timeline of the project itself. Because of the condensed timeline we started designing the physical system before we had a fully flushed model. This meant ideal parameters of our system, hip mass, swing mass, and leg length, had not been fully optimized prior to starting the CAD. Additionally, due to uncalculated forces in the model that would appear in the real world, we wanted to ensure that the physical walker allowed for some variance in build to account for the models imperfections.

When designing the physical walker we made design decisions based on the assumptions we made while designing the model. In creating the physical model we had assumed that the legs would have a negligible mass compared to the mass of the hip. We also assumed that, since our model was done in 2D, the mass of the hip and swing foot would be evenly distributed along the 3D axis.

Additionally, order to account for the variance between the model and the physical world we designed the legs of the walker to be adjustable and the feet to be interchangeable so we could test different parameters that had worker in our simulation in the real world. We designed the walker with a rough set of parameters, laser our design, and manufactured the walker before moving forward with accuracy evaluation and physical testing.

Validation

We validated our system multiple ways. For the model we validated it by testing extreme cases for our parameters. These consistently produced results that we expected and we therefore thought our model was valid. We also validated our model by checking the conservation of energy of our system using the Hamiltonian equation (Figure ) this faithfully alerted us when our physics wasn’t correct and made it easy to find broken spots in the code.

We were able to use graphs of the predicted angles and angular velocities to develop an understanding of what our model was doing. By predicting where the collisions for the walker should be and the trajectory of the angles we were able to validate the physical system that we created. Additionally, we were able to validate our model against the analysis of other passive walkers, to prove that our results were reasonable.

We additionally were able to build a physical model of our system. We tested this physical system against our model and were able to see very similar performance between both! When we set the physical model’s parameters to parameters that failed in our model the system failed in the same way, at the same time (Figure and ). Additionally, we were able to produce a simulation that took multiple steps using parameters from our physical system that could also take multiple steps.
